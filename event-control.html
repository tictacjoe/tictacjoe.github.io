Event Control

The concept of "event control" comes from a desire to describe the goals of cyber security in a broad, jargon-less way. It also reflects that cyber security should entail more than just "knowledge" of events but should be about using this knowledge to exercise control over the events that occur on our systems. There is a growing recognition of this in the industry, under names like "Managed Detection Response", "Automated Response", etc., however it is often overlooked that automating real actions in the real world requires a degree of certainty that our analystics currently struggle to provide for all but the simplest of cases. In some ways it seems that Managed Detection Response is sought out as a way to compensate for the inadequaciees of analysis ("we've got to save time somewhere"), when it is actually highly dependent on the accuracy of analysis to be of any value.

To me, the basics of effective cyber security are fairly simple: 1) know whats on your network, 2) know whats happening, 3) investigate anything unusual, 4) stop what shouldn't be happening.

This was attainable in the past or with small, simple networks with assets numbering in the hundreds, and good, thorough management could keep assets and processes completely inventoried. Now, however, for most organizations there is no hope of accurately knowing exactly what happening on their network, or even what devices they really have on their networks. To my mind this is the greatest promise of AI applications for cyber security -- the ability to approximate "normal" within unknown systems with incomplete data sets. Most of the efforts I've pursued have been under this idea. It really goes back to the basics of inventory and baselining, but utilizing AI to approximate the gaps in knowledge that we need.
