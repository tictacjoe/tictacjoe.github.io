Event Control

The phrase I sometimes use to describe my work in cyber security ("event control") comes from a desire to describe the goals of cyber security in a broad, jargon-less way that reflects the use of event and correlation analysis to exercise control over system events. There is a growing recognition of the demand for this in the industry, under names like "Managed Detection Response", "Automated Response", etc., however it is often overlooked that automating real actions in the real world requires a degree of certainty that our analystics currently struggle to provide for all but the simplest of cases. In some ways it seems that Managed Detection Response is sought out as a way to compensate for the inadequaciees of analysis ("we've got to save time somewhere"), when it is actually highly dependent on the accuracy of analysis to be of any value.

To me, the basics of effective cyber security are fairly simple: 1) know what's on your network, 2) know what's happening, 3) investigate anything unusual, 4) stop what shouldn't be happening.

This was attainable in the past or with small, simple networks with assets numbering in the hundreds, when good, thorough management could keep assets and processes completely inventoried. Now however, for most organizations there is no hope of accurately knowing exactly what happening on their network, or even what assets they actually have on their networks. To my mind this is the greatest promise of AI applications for cyber security -- the ability to approximate "normal" within unknown systems with incomplete data sets. Many of the efforts I've pursued over the past couple of years have been related to this idea. It really goes back to the basics of inventory and baselining, but utilizing AI to approximate the gaps in knowledge that we need.
